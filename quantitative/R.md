# R for Social Science Research

## Overview

R is an open-source statistical computing environment widely used in social sciences for data analysis, visualization, reproducible research, and advanced statistical modeling. It is especially strong in:

- Reproducible workflows
- Data visualization
- Multilevel modeling
- Structural equation modeling
- Bayesian analysis
- Large-scale data processing

R is highly recommended for PhD-level research and publication-oriented projects.

---

## Why Use R in Social Science?

### Strengths

- Free and open-source
- Extensive package ecosystem (CRAN)
- Excellent visualization (ggplot2)
- Strong support for reproducible research (R Markdown, Quarto)
- Advanced statistical modeling capabilities
- Active academic community

### Limitations

- Steeper learning curve for beginners
- Requires basic coding knowledge
- Less menu-driven compared to SPSS

## Recommended Academic Books

1. Wickham, H., & Grolemund, G. (2017). *R for Data Science*. O’Reilly.  
   → Practical introduction to data manipulation and visualization.

2. Fox, J., & Weisberg, S. (2019). *An R Companion to Applied Regression*. Sage.  
   → Strong applied regression resource for social sciences.

3. Gelman, A., Hill, J., & Vehtari, A. (2020). *Regression and Other Stories*. Cambridge University Press.

## Reproducibility Example

Example workflow:

1. Import data (`readr`)
2. Clean data (`dplyr`)
3. Visualize trends (`ggplot2`)
4. Run regression (`lm()` or `lme4`)
5. Export results via R Markdown

---

## When to Choose R Over Other Tools

Choose R when:

- You need reproducibility
- You are publishing in high-impact journals
- You require advanced modeling
- You want strong visualization
- You are working on a PhD or academic career path
